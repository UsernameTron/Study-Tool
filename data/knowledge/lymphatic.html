Lymphatic System

Overview

The lymphatic system is a network of vessels, organs, and tissues that help maintain fluid balance in the body and defend against infections.

Key Components

  • Lymph: Clear fluid that contains white blood cells, particularly lymphocytes.
  • Lymph vessels: Network of vessels that transport lymph throughout the body.
  • Lymph nodes: Small, bean-shaped organs located along lymph vessels that filter lymph and trap pathogens.
  • Spleen: Largest lymphatic organ, filters blood and stores white blood cells.
  • Thymus: Where T cells mature, located in the upper chest.
  • Tonsils: Trap pathogens entering through the mouth and nose.

Functions

  1. Maintains fluid balance by collecting excess fluid and returning it to the bloodstream.
  2. Absorbs fats and fat-soluble vitamins from the digestive system.
  3. Defends the body against infection by producing white blood cells and antibodies.

Histology

Understanding the microanatomy of lymphatic tissues is crucial for comprehending their function:

Lymph Node Histology

Lymph nodes have a distinct capsule and are organized into:

  • Cortex: Contains follicles with germinal centers (B cell areas)
  • Paracortex: T cell-rich area
  • Medulla: Contains medullary cords and sinuses
  • Afferent and Efferent Lymphatics: For lymph flow in and out of the node

The histology slide shows these structures with germinal centers appearing as lighter-stained circular areas within the darker cortex.

Thymus Histology

The thymus has a distinctive lobular structure with:

  • Cortex: Dense population of developing T cells (thymocytes)
  • Medulla: Less densely populated with mature T cells
  • Hassall's Corpuscles: Distinctive whorled structures in the medulla

The thymus is essential for T cell development and maturation.

Spleen Histology

The spleen shows a clear distinction between:

  • White Pulp: Lymphoid tissue surrounding arterioles
  • Red Pulp: Blood-filled sinuses where old red blood cells are broken down
  • Marginal Zone: Transition between white and red pulp

The spleen serves both immune and blood filtering functions.

Clinical Significance

Disorders of the lymphatic system include:

  • Lymphedema: Swelling due to lymph fluid buildup.
  • Lymphadenopathy: Enlarged lymph nodes due to infection or cancer.
  • Lymphoma: Cancer of the lymphatic system that can be identified through histological examination showing disrupted lymph node architecture.

Laboratory Examination

Histological samples of lymphatic tissues are typically stained with H&E (Hematoxylin and Eosin) to visualize cellular structures. Special stains and immunohistochemistry may be used to identify specific cell types and markers relevant to diagnosis.
