Digestive System

Overview

The digestive system processes food, extracts nutrients, and eliminates waste. It consists of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract and accessory organs.

Key Components

  • Mouth: Beginning of the GI tract where mechanical and chemical digestion starts.
  • Pharynx: Common passageway for food and air.
  • Esophagus: Muscular tube that connects the pharynx to the stomach.
  • Stomach: J-shaped organ that mixes food with digestive enzymes and acid.
  • Small intestine: Divided into duodenum, jejunum, and ileum; primary site of nutrient absorption.
  • Large intestine: Absorbs water and electrolytes; forms and stores feces.
  • Rectum and anus: Final portions of the digestive tract for waste elimination.

Accessory Organs

  • Liver: Produces bile for fat digestion; processes nutrients; detoxifies harmful substances.
  • Gallbladder: Stores and concentrates bile from the liver.
  • Pancreas: Produces digestive enzymes and hormones (insulin and glucagon).
  • Salivary glands: Produce saliva containing amylase to begin carbohydrate digestion.

Digestive Processes

  1. Ingestion: Taking food into the mouth.
  2. Propulsion: Movement of food through the GI tract via peristalsis.
  3. Mechanical digestion: Physical breakdown of food by chewing and churning.
  4. Chemical digestion: Breakdown of food by enzymes and acids.
  5. Absorption: Transfer of nutrients from the GI tract to the bloodstream.
  6. Defecation: Elimination of undigested material as feces.

Clinical Significance

Common digestive disorders include:

  • GERD: Gastroesophageal reflux disease, where stomach acid flows back into the esophagus.
  • Peptic ulcers: Open sores in the stomach or duodenal lining.
  • IBD: Inflammatory bowel disease, including Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis.
  • Gallstones: Hardened deposits in the gallbladder.
  • Cirrhosis: Scarring of the liver tissue.
