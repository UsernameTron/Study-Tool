Respiratory System

Overview

The respiratory system is responsible for gas exchange between the body and the external environment, bringing oxygen into the body and removing carbon dioxide.

Key Components

  • Nose and nasal cavity: Filters, warms, and humidifies air.
  • Pharynx (throat): Passageway for both air and food.
  • Larynx (voice box): Contains vocal cords and helps protect the airway.
  • Trachea (windpipe): Tube that connects the larynx to the bronchi.
  • Bronchi and bronchioles: Branches of airways that lead to the alveoli.
  • Lungs: Main organs of respiration containing alveoli where gas exchange occurs.
  • Diaphragm: Main muscle of respiration that contracts to create negative pressure in the thoracic cavity.

Lung Divisions

Right Lung: Divided into three lobes - superior, middle, and inferior.

Left Lung: Divided into two lobes - superior and inferior (has a cardiac notch to accommodate the heart).

Respiration Process

  1. Ventilation: Movement of air into and out of the lungs.
  2. External respiration: Gas exchange between alveoli and pulmonary capillaries.
  3. Transport: Movement of gases through the bloodstream.
  4. Internal respiration: Gas exchange between systemic capillaries and tissue cells.

Histology

Understanding the microscopic structure of respiratory tissues is essential for comprehending respiratory physiology:

Trachea Histology

The trachea has several distinct layers:

  • Mucosa: Contains pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium with goblet cells
  • Submucosa: Contains seromucous glands
  • Cartilage: C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings that provide structural support
  • Adventitia: Outer fibrous layer

The cilia in the epithelium beat in coordinated waves to move mucus and trapped particles up toward the pharynx (mucociliary escalator).

Lung Histology

The lung parenchyma shows progressive branching of the respiratory tree:

  • Bronchi: Similar structure to trachea but with smaller diameter and cartilage plates instead of rings
  • Bronchioles: No cartilage, simple columnar to cuboidal epithelium, smooth muscle layer
  • Terminal Bronchioles: Cuboidal epithelium with Clara cells
  • Respiratory Bronchioles: Transition zone with some alveoli in walls
  • Alveolar Ducts and Alveoli: Simple squamous epithelium (Type I pneumocytes) with Type II pneumocytes that produce surfactant

The alveolar-capillary membrane, where gas exchange occurs, is extremely thin (0.5-1.0 Î¼m).

Clinical Significance

Common respiratory conditions include:

  • Asthma: Chronic inflammatory disease causing airway narrowing and obstruction.
  • COPD: Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, including emphysema and chronic bronchitis.
  • Pneumonia: Infection causing inflammation of the alveoli.
  • Lung cancer: Uncontrolled growth of abnormal cells in the lung that can be classified histologically into different types (adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, small cell carcinoma, etc.).

Laboratory Examination

Respiratory tissue samples are typically examined using:

  • H&E Staining: Provides general tissue architecture
  • PAS Staining: Highlights mucin-producing cells
  • Masson's Trichrome: Shows collagen distribution in fibrotic diseases
  • Immunohistochemistry: Used to identify specific cell types and markers for diagnostic purposes

Bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) may also be used to sample cells and fluid from the lower respiratory tract for cytological examination.
